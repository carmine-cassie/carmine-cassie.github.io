What I Read this Month!

Thirteen Storeys

A couple years ago I listened to The Magnus Archives, which I loved! I'm usually really bad with horror, but only in movies, apparently, where the artists can make me uncomfortable through the shortcut of my prey instincts. Audio and written horror I'm pretty chill with, it turns out, so I very happily got through The Magnus Archives. I've always been vaguely aware that Jonathan Sims, the show's writer, had written a couple physical books, and so this month I got around to his first entry!

The thing with Thirteen Storeys, though, is that it's basically just The Magnus Archives - it's a collection of short stories that slowly weave together into a bigger picture. I love Sims' particular fascination with peoples' way of life, the start of each story where you settle into the narrator's niche in the world is a joy to read. I also liked the way that I'd recognise background characters as obvious protagonists of other stories in the anthology, particular the way that once I was halfway through the book, I'd start recognising more characters than not.

I did find the book a bit, hm, "clawless". Each horror story culminated in a bit of an anticlimactic ending, and the book's dramatic final ending was a bit cheesy, it felt. I did really like, though, how the ending's big reveal was an explanation of how all the book's horrors were strung along a cohesive theme. Also loved the growing sense of dread throughout most of the stories, like in the first chapter, when she kept mentioning her calloused, soil-stained hands I got so freaked out!

I'm excited to try out his other books, to see what he does in a non-anthological format - like I say, I enjoy how he writes his characters, so I'm curious what a Jonathan Sims character with a full novel's worth of depth is like!

Twig Arc 17: Gut Feeling

Really good arc, though we're in the endgame so my thoughts here are gonna be pretty spoilery, hey! The, uh, arc of this arc feels like an inversion of Arc 15 in a lot of ways. Where that one led with a rambunctious fun time before tumbling down when Jessie drops a memory, here we're overwhelmed on the return of The Lambs by how bad of a shape they're in. Only at the very end when Sy kisses Jessie do we feel like anything might be alright. It's nice to be surprised with a bit of hope like that, even though all else remains ever dire. Like, oh my goodness, the part where the chapter opens with Sy having a full conversation with Helen and Gordon Two, before realising they're not real and he doesn't quite remember how he got where he is! Really effective use of the discontinuity of the chapter boundary to portray dissociation, super scary and gut-dropping , really good stuff.

I'm in a time in my life where, like, the central metaphor the piece is exploring in this arc is really hitting me, that equation of 'growing up into an adult' == 'dying slowly and horribly, because you weren't built to survive in this world'. Hopefully the (fast-approaching) ending provides some closure! But who knows, Wildbow endings can be quite a toss-up, it seems.

Like, on that note, I was remarking elsewhere that Twig feels like it loses a bit of steam in the back third, because its strongest attribute is the lovely, rich characters, and it keeps killing them off and isolating them, and I was thankful that The Lambs had kinda reunited, what with Helen joining Sy and Jessie, and then literally in the next chapter I read, Helen gets riddled with bullets! I ate my words immediately, dude.
